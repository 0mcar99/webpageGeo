GLOBAL CONFLICT COMPASS

A bold, structured view of world conflicts: who is aligned with whom, where tensions are rising, what states intend to protect, and where the international system is heading next.

Current Conflict Situation

  • Russia-Ukraine: A long war of territory, security architecture, and military endurance.
  • Israel-Gaza and regional fronts: Humanitarian crisis plus wider escalation risks in the Middle East.
  • Taiwan Strait and South China Sea: Strategic competition, maritime signaling, and high-stakes deterrence.
  • Red Sea and shipping lanes: Non-state attacks disrupt trade routes and raise global logistics costs.
  • Sahel and Horn of Africa: Internal instability intersects with external power competition.
  • Cyber and information warfare: Attacks now target data, finance, elections, and public trust.

How Countries Interact and Deal with Each Other

Diplomacy

Negotiation channels, crisis hotlines, and backchannel talks reduce the risk of miscalculation.

Deterrence

Military posture, joint exercises, and defense pacts are used to communicate red lines.

Economic Statecraft

Sanctions, export controls, debt deals, and energy contracts now function as strategic weapons.

Institutions and Law

UN forums, regional blocs, and treaties still shape legitimacy and limits, even in hard rivalry.

Dot Connections: One Crisis Triggers Another

  1. Energy chokepoints raise prices, which feeds inflation and domestic political stress.
  2. Technology restrictions split supply chains and accelerate rival industrial blocs.
  3. Food and fertilizer shocks increase migration pressure and social unrest.
  4. Cyber sabotage lowers trust and pushes countries toward harsher security doctrines.
  5. Proxy conflicts cross regions, turning local wars into global pressure systems.

Intentions and Where the World Is Heading

Near Term (1-3 Years)

More fragmented alignments, selective cooperation, and persistent grey-zone confrontation.

Mid Term (3-7 Years)

Defense spending remains high, technology blocs harden, and strategic autonomy deepens.

Long Term (7+ Years)

A multipolar order normalizes issue-based coalitions, constant bargaining, and limited trust.

Immunity and Strategic Resilience: Against What?

  • Sanction immunity: Alternative payment channels, local-currency trade, and reserve diversification.
  • Supply-chain immunity: Friend-shoring, near-shoring, and critical mineral partnerships.
  • Cyber immunity: Redundant infrastructure, national CERT systems, and fast incident response.
  • Narrative immunity: Counter-disinformation systems and transparent public communication.
  • Climate-security immunity: Water planning, disaster readiness, and resilient food systems.
